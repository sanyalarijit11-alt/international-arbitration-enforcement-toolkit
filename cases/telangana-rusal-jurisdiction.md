# Interim Relief Is Not Enforcement: Telangana HC on Jurisdictional Limits in Multi-Forum Award Enforcement

## Core Lesson

The Telangana High Court's decision in *OWH SE I.L. v. United Company Rusal* is a useful reminder that obtaining interim protective orders in transnational enforcement proceedings does not guarantee that the enforcing court will ultimately sustain jurisdiction over the execution proceedings themselves.

The award holder sought enforcement of LCIA awards across multiple jurisdictions and attempted to proceed against alleged alter ego entities of the award debtor. The petitioner successfully obtained interim protection restraining transfer of shares linked to an Indian company structure. However, despite accepting at a prima facie stage that the execution petition may be maintainable against the group entities, the Court ultimately dismissed the proceedings for lack of territorial jurisdiction.

## Why the Case Matters

The decision demonstrates an important distinction in enforcement strategy:

* Interim relief may be granted on a prima facie assessment designed to preserve assets.
* Final enforcement jurisdiction requires a far stricter examination of situs, corporate structure, and territorial nexus.

The Court accepted that alter ego and veil-piercing arguments could, in principle, permit impleadment of non-signatory affiliates in foreign award enforcement proceedings, relying heavily on *Gemini Bay* and *Imax Corporation*.

However, the decisive issue ultimately became territorial jurisdiction.

## The Jurisdictional Problem

The award holder argued that the Telangana High Court had jurisdiction because the shareholder register of Pioneer Aluminium Industries Ltd. was maintained in Hyderabad, even though:

* the company’s registered office was in Andhra Pradesh;
* the shares belonged to a Qatar-incorporated entity; and
* related proceedings had already been initiated in Qatar.

The Telangana Court rejected this approach.

It held that:

* the situs of the relevant shares was elsewhere 
* jurisdiction could not be founded merely on the location of shareholder records/books; and
* under company law principles, the more relevant nexus remained the registered office jurisdiction, which would make the High Court of Andhra Pradesh more appropriate.

As a result, the execution petition was dismissed and the interim injunction vacated.

## Practical Takeaway

For award creditors, the case is a reminder that:

* multi-jurisdiction enforcement strategy must be tied to sustainable jurisdictional anchors, not merely identifiable commercial touchpoints;
* interim anti-transfer or asset preservation orders should not be mistaken for validation of final enforcement jurisdiction; and
* alter ego theories may open the door to impleading affiliates, but they do not eliminate the need for a robust territorial nexus to the enforcing court.

The decision also illustrates the increasing procedural complexity of modern award enforcement campaigns involving:

* sanctions-related restructuring,
* layered corporate entities,
* cross-border shareholding structures, and
* parallel proceedings in multiple jurisdictions.

In practice, aggressive enforcement strategy still depends on choosing the right forum, not merely finding a reachable asset.
